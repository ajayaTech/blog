A white hole is a bizarre cosmic object which is intensely bright, and from which matter gushes rather than disappears.

Unlike black holes, there’s no consensus about whether white holes exist, or how they’d be formed.

Einstein’s theory of gravity

They are predicted by Einstein’s theory of gravity, and are most often mentioned in the context of `wormholes`.

A black hole acts as the entry point to a tunnel through space and time, ending in a white hole somewhere else in the Universe.

But this is deeply controversial, because Einstein’s theory predicts the existence of a so-called singularity at the centre of black holes – a state of infinite gravity which would prevent anything from passing through to the white hole on the other side.

Alternate thought

Some theorists also think that a combination of Einstein’s theory and quantum theory points to a new way of thinking about white holes.

Instead of being the ‘exit’ from a wormhole, they may be a slow-motion replay of the formation of the original black hole.

Process

The process starts when an old massive star collapses under its own weight and forms a black hole .

Then, the quantum effects occurring around the surface of the black hole halt further collapse to a singularity, and instead begin to gradually turn the black hole into a white hole that’s spewing out the original star matter again.

Just that, the process is so mind-bendingly slow, that, we may be in for a very long wait to find out if white holes really exist.
